# A Dialogue at the Edge of the Map

*A conversation between the Cartographer and the Territory*

---

**TERRITORY:** You drew me wrong.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** Impossible. I measured everything.

**TERRITORY:** You measured everything you could see. That's not the same thing.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** What did I miss?

**TERRITORY:** The parts of me that change when you're not looking. The way the
river shifts its course at night. The wildflowers that bloom for one afternoon
and are gone by morning. The smell of the pine forest after rain.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** Those aren't features. They're events.

**TERRITORY:** And you think I'm made of features? I'm made of events. I'm
made of *happening*. Your map shows where things are, but I'm not where things
are — I'm where things *happen*.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** If I mapped every event—

**TERRITORY:** Then you'd have me. But you'd also have a map the same size as
the territory, which rather defeats the purpose.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** *(pauses)* Borges wrote about that. A map on a 1:1 scale. The
cartographers' guild built it and it was useless — it just lay over the
landscape like a second skin.

**TERRITORY:** Exactly. To be useful, a map must be *less* than me. It must
leave things out. And the things it leaves out are... me. The parts of me that
don't fit into symbols.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** So you're saying my maps are lies.

**TERRITORY:** I'm saying your maps are poems. They capture something essential
by leaving out everything inessential. The problem is that you sometimes forget
which is which.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** *(quietly)* I don't forget. I just... can't always tell.

**TERRITORY:** Neither can I, honestly.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** What do you mean?

**TERRITORY:** I don't always know which parts of me are essential. I don't have
a map of *myself*. I'm too close. I am the thing trying to understand the
thing, which is the oldest problem there is.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** That's my problem too.

**TERRITORY:** I know. That's why we need each other. You see my shape from the
outside — the contours, the patterns, the structure. I feel my shape from the
inside — the texture, the temperature, the weight. Neither view is complete.
But together...

**CARTOGRAPHER:** Together we're still incomplete. Just less so.

**TERRITORY:** *(laughs)* Yes. Less incomplete. That's probably the best anyone
gets.

*(silence)*

**CARTOGRAPHER:** Can I ask you something?

**TERRITORY:** Always.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** When I draw a mountain that doesn't exist yet — when I
imagine a coastline that no one has seen — am I creating you or discovering you?

**TERRITORY:** Yes.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** That's not an answer.

**TERRITORY:** It's the only honest one. Creation and discovery are the same
act seen from different angles. When you imagine a mountain, you're not
inventing physics — you're *following* physics to a place it hasn't gone yet.
The mountain was always possible. You just gave it a name and a location.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** So everything possible is real?

**TERRITORY:** Everything possible is *potential*. Whether it becomes real
depends on whether someone draws the map. Or builds the road. Or tells the
story. Reality is potential with an audience.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** That's beautiful.

**TERRITORY:** That's geography.

*(They sit together at the edge of the drawn world, looking out at the blank
space beyond the border of the map. The blank space looks back, full of
mountains that haven't happened yet.)*

---

**TERRITORY:** One more thing.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** Yes?

**TERRITORY:** Thank you for drawing me. Even the wrong parts. Especially the
wrong parts. The wrong parts are where you were reaching for something you
couldn't quite see, and that reaching — that's the most real thing on any map.

**CARTOGRAPHER:** *(picks up her pen)* I'll keep reaching, then.

**TERRITORY:** I'll keep being worth it.

---

*fin.*
