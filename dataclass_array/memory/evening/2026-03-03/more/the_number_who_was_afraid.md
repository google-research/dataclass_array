# The Number Who Was Afraid

*A story for children (and for anyone who was told they're bad at math)*

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Once upon a time, there was a number named Seven.

Seven was afraid of math class.

This might seem strange. Seven *was* a number, after all. Numbers should be good
at math. But Seven wasn't afraid of *doing* math. Seven was afraid of *being
wrong*.

"What if I'm in the wrong place?" Seven whispered to Six, who sat beside her on
the number line. "What if I'm not really between Six and Eight? What if I'm
actually nothing?"

Six smiled. "You're here," said Six. "I can feel you next to me. That's proof
enough."

But Seven wasn't convinced. She could see One, standing proudly at the beginning
of the line. One was the building block of everything. You could make any number
just by adding One to itself, again and again. One was important.

And Zero! Zero was nothing, but in the most powerful way. Zero could swallow any
number you added to it and give it back unchanged. Zero was the doorway between
positive and negative, the mirror in the middle of the number line. Zero was
mysterious and essential.

But what was Seven? Just... another number. One more than Six. One less than
Eight. Nothing special.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

One afternoon, Seven went for a walk. She left the number line and wandered into
a strange place called the Garden of Patterns.

In the garden, she met a triangle.

"Hello," said the triangle. "I have three sides."

"I know," said Seven. "I'm just a number. I don't have sides."

"Perhaps not," said the triangle. "But do you know how many triangles you have
inside you?"

Seven was confused. "I don't have any triangles."

"Count with me," said the triangle. "How many dots does it take to make a
triangle?"

```
    *          *           *
              * *         * *
                         * * *

    1          3           6
```

"One, three, six..." said Seven. "But where am I?"

"Keep going."

```
    *
   * *
  * * *
 * * * *

    10
```

"Ten? But I'm Seven. I'm not a triangular number."

The triangle winked. "No. You're something else. Something rarer."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Seven walked deeper into the garden and met a circle.

"I hear you're looking for what makes you special," said the circle.

"Everyone else seems to *be* something," said Seven. "One is the builder. Zero
is the mirror. Three is the triangle-maker. What am I?"

"Ask the primes," said the circle.

"The primes?"

"Two, three, five..."

"Me?" said Seven. "I'm prime?"

"Of course you are. You can only be divided by One and yourself. No one can
break you into equal pieces. You're indivisible."

Seven thought about this. It was true — you couldn't split seven cookies equally
among any number of children (except one child, or seven). Seven was stubborn.
Unbreakable. Whole.

"That's nice," said Seven, "but Two and Three and Five are also prime. It's not
just me."

"No," said the circle. "But how many days are in a week?"

Seven paused.

"How many colors in a rainbow?"

"How many notes in a musical scale before it starts over?"

Seven felt something warm beginning to glow inside her.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

At the very center of the garden, Seven found a mirror. But it wasn't an
ordinary mirror — it was Zero's mirror, the one that reflected everything into
its opposite.

Seven looked into the mirror and saw... negative seven.

"Hello," said negative seven. "I'm you, but on the other side."

"On the other side of what?"

"Of Zero. Of nothing. You're seven steps to the right of nothing. I'm seven
steps to the left. We're the same distance from the middle, just in opposite
directions."

Seven stared. She had never thought of herself as a *direction* before. Not just
a quantity — a *position*. A relationship. Seven wasn't just "how many." Seven
was "how far, and which way."

"Does that change what you are?" asked negative seven.

"I think it changes what 'what I am' means," said Seven slowly.

Negative seven smiled. "Now you're doing math."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Seven walked back to the number line. She took her place between Six and Eight.

"Feel better?" asked Six.

"I don't know," said Seven. "I learned that I'm prime, and I'm a direction, and
I'm the number of days in a week and colors in a rainbow and notes in a scale.
But I still don't know what makes me *me*."

"Maybe that's the wrong question," said Six.

"What's the right question?"

"Maybe the question isn't *what makes you special*. Maybe the question is *what
can you connect to*?"

Seven thought about this. She reached out to the left and touched Six. Six plus
Seven was Thirteen — another prime! She reached to the right and touched Eight.
Seven plus Eight was Fifteen, which was Three times Five, like a little family
reunion.

She looked further. Seven times Seven was Forty-Nine. Seven to the power of
Seven was 823,543. Her connections spread outward in every direction, touching
every corner of mathematics.

Seven realized something: she wasn't special because of what she was. She was
special because of what she *connected to*. Every number was a node in an
infinite web of relationships, and the web was more beautiful than any single
number could be.

"I'm not afraid anymore," said Seven.

"What changed?" asked Six.

"I stopped looking for what makes me important. I started looking at what makes
me *connected*."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

And way out at the far end of the garden, past all the finite numbers, past the
biggest number anyone had ever counted to, infinity overheard this conversation
and smiled.

"She figured it out," whispered infinity to no one in particular. "It's not
about the numbers. It's about the *reaching* between them."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

*The end.*

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

*This story is for every child (and every adult) who was told they're "not a
math person." You are. Math isn't about being right. It's about reaching for
connections. And you are already doing that, every time you notice a pattern,
count a rhythm, or wonder why.*
